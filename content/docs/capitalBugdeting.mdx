## 1. Introduction to Capital Budgeting

- **Definition:** The process of evaluating and selecting long-term investments (like machinery, plants, or projects) that yield returns over a period of time.
- **Objective:** Maximize shareholder wealth (i.e., only accept projects that increase the company's value).
- **Characteristics:** 
  - Huge initial outlay (lumpy investments).
  - Long-term commitment (irreversible once done).
  - High risk (future is uncertain).

---

## 2. Basic Concepts (Must Master Before Solving)

- **Initial Investment ($I_0$):** Total cost to start the project (purchase + installation). This is a **Cash Outflow** at Year 0.
- **Cash Inflows (CF):** Net income + Depreciation (since depreciation is a non-cash expense). These are **positive** (+).
- **Cash Outflows:** Operating costs, maintenance, and initial investment. These are **negative** (-).
- **Net Cash Flow:** Inflow - Outflow for a given year.
- **Salvage Value:** Scrap / resale value at the end of the project's life. It is a **positive Cash Inflow** at Year \(n\).
- **Discount Rate (r / MARR):** The minimum return the company expects (e.g., 10%). Also called **Cost of Capital**.
- **Project Life (n):** Expected operational years of the asset.
- **Useful Life vs Economic Life:** Useful life is the accounting estimate; economic life is the period where the asset generates optimal profits.
- **Incremental Cash Flows:** The *extra* cash flow generated by choosing one project over another (used in Replacement Analysis).
- **Cash Flow Sign Convention:** Normal pattern is `- + + + +` (one outflow, then all inflows). If signs change multiple times (e.g., `- + - +`), we get **Multiple IRRs** – in that case, always trust **NPV**.

> **Crucial Concept:** We solve these numerically by **drawing a timeline**. Put the Initial Cost at Year 0 (negative cash flow) and the returns in Year 1, 2,... n (positive).

---

## 3. Types of Investment Decisions

| Type | Meaning | Exam Focus |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Independent** | Accepting Project A doesn't affect Project B. | Accept both if they meet criteria. |
| **Mutually Exclusive** | You must choose ONE out of many (e.g., buying Machine X or Y). | **Highest NPV wins.** |
| **Replacement** | Replace old equipment with new. | Focus on incremental cash flows. |
| **Expansion** | Increase existing capacity. | Standard analysis applies. |

---

## 4. Additional Theory Topics (Mandatory for Full Marks)

- **Project Evaluation and Selection:** The process of analyzing potential projects to determine which ones provide the best financial return and align with strategic goals.
- **Potential Difficulties:** 
  - **Uncertainty:** Future cash flows are estimates, not guarantees.
  - **Mutually Exclusive Conflict:** NPV and IRR may give different rankings.
  - **Capital Rationing:** Limited budget prevents taking all profitable projects.
- **Capital Rationing:** A situation where a company has a fixed budget and cannot invest in all positive NPV projects. **Solution:** Rank projects by Profitability Index (PI) and select the combination with the highest total NPV.
- **Project Monitoring:** Tracking the project's actual performance during its construction or operation phases to catch cost overruns early.
- **Post-Completion Audit:** A review conducted after the project is complete to compare actual results (cash flows, costs) with the estimated projections. It holds managers accountable and improves future forecasting.

# 5. Payback Period (PBP) ⭐⭐⭐

**Definition:** The time required to recover the original investment from the net cash inflows.

**Decision Rule:** *Shorter* is *Better*. Compare to a predetermined cutoff period.

---

### Case 1: Equal Annual Cash Flows

**Formula:** 
$$ \text{Payback} = \frac{\text{Initial Investment}}{\text{Annual Cash Inflow}} $$

**Example 1 (Equal CFs)**

> **Question:** A project costs $50,000 and generates $12,500 annually for 6 years. Find Payback.
> **Solution:**
> $$ PBP = \frac{50,000}{12,500} = 4 \text{ years} $$
> *Final Answer: 4 Years.*

---

### Case 2: Unequal Cash Flows

**Approach:** Use the **Cumulative Cash Flow** method.

**Example 2 (Unequal CFs)**

> **Question:** Project costs $45,000. Cash flows: Y1=10,000, Y2=20,000, Y3=15,000, Y4=5,000. Find Payback.

**Solution Table:**

| Year | Cash Flow | Cumulative Cash Flow | Status |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0 | -45,000 | -45,000 | Pending |
| 1 | +10,000 | -35,000 | Pending |
| 2 | +20,000 | -15,000 | Pending |
| 3 | +15,000 | **0** | **Recovered!** |

> **Final Answer:** Exactly **3 years**.

---

### Example 3 (Fractional Payback - Exam Favorite)

> **Question:** Investment = $1,00,000. CFs: Y1=20,000; Y2=30,000; Y3=40,000; Y4=30,000.

**Solution:**
| Year | CF | Cumulative |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | 20,000 | -80,000 |
| 2 | 30,000 | -50,000 |
| 3 | 40,000 | -10,000 (Recovered in Y4) |

- Amount needed at start of Year 4 = **$10,000**
- Cash flow in Year 4 = **$30,000**
- Fraction = $10,000 / 30,000 = 0.33

$$ PBP = 3 + 0.33 = 3.33 \text{ years} $$

> **Final Answer: 3.33 Years.**

**Pros:** Simple, ignores TVM. **Cons:** Ignores cash flows after payback.

# 6. Discounted Payback Period (DPP) ⭐⭐⭐

**Definition:** Time to recover the investment using *discounted* (Present Value) cash flows.

**Decision Rule:** Shorter is better. (More realistic than PBP).

**Key Difference:** PBP uses raw cash; DPP uses PV of cash.

**Example (Discounted Payback)**

> **Question:** Investment = $50,000. CFs: Y1=20,000; Y2=25,000; Y3=15,000. Discount rate = 10%.

**Step 1:** Discount each Cash Flow to Year 0.
- PV₁ = 20,000 / (1.1)¹ = **18,181**
- PV₂ = 25,000 / (1.1)² = 25,000 / 1.21 = **20,661**
- PV₃ = 15,000 / (1.1)³ = 15,000 / 1.331 = **11,270**

**Step 2:** Cumulative PV Table.
| Year | PV of CF | Cumulative PV |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0 | - | -50,000 |
| 1 | 18,181 | -31,819 |
| 2 | 20,661 | -11,158 |
| 3 | 11,270 | **+112** (Recovered) |

- Amount needed at start of Y3 = $11,158
- CF in Y3 = $11,270
- Fraction = 11,158 / 11,270 = 0.99

$$ DPP = 2 + 0.99 = 2.99 \approx 3 \text{ years} $$

# 7. Net Present Value (NPV) ⭐⭐⭐

**Definition:** The total value added (or subtracted) to the firm by undertaking a project. It measures the absolute surplus generated.

**Formula:** 
$$ NPV = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{CF_t}{(1+r)^t} - \text{Initial Investment} $$

**Decision Rule:** 
- **NPV > 0** → Accept (Project earns MORE than required return).
- **NPV < 0** → Reject.
- **NPV = 0** → Indifferent (exactly meets required return).

**Example (Standard NPV)**

> **Question:** Initial cost = $100,000. Cash flows: Y1=40,000; Y2=50,000; Y3=30,000. Discount Rate = 10%.

**Solution:**

$$ NPV = \frac{40,000}{(1.1)^1} + \frac{50,000}{(1.1)^2} + \frac{30,000}{(1.1)^3} - 100,000 $$

$$ NPV = \frac{40,000}{1.1} + \frac{50,000}{1.21} + \frac{30,000}{1.331} - 100,000 $$

$$ NPV = 36,363.64 + 41,322.31 + 22,539.44 - 100,000 $$

$$ NPV = 100,225.39 - 100,000 = 225.39 $$

> **Final Answer:** NPV = **$225.39**. Since > 0, **Accept the project**.

# 8. Internal Rate of Return (IRR) ⭐⭐⭐

**Definition:** The discount rate that makes the **NPV equal to zero**. It is the actual percentage return earned by the project.

**Concept:** 
$$ \text{Find } IRR \text{ such that } \sum \frac{CF_t}{(1+IRR)^t} = \text{Initial Investment} $$

**Decision Rule:** If IRR > MARR (required rate) → **Accept**. If IRR < MARR → **Reject**.

---

### The "Trial & Error + Interpolation" Method (Exam Heavy)

You cannot solve IRR directly; you guess two rates and interpolate.

**Formula for Interpolation:**
$$ IRR = R_L + \frac{NPV_L}{NPV_L - NPV_H} \times (R_H - R_L) $$

*(Where L = Lower rate, H = Higher rate).*

---

**Example (IRR Calculation)**

> **Question:** Initial Cost = $100,000. CFs: Y1=40,000; Y2=50,000; Y3=30,000. Find IRR.

*(Note: This is the exact same project as the NPV example. We found NPV at 10% was +225).*

**Step 1:** We need a Positive NPV and a Negative NPV.
- At 10% (Low Rate), NPV_L = **+$225**.
- Try a Higher rate (say 11%).

**Step 2:** Calculate NPV at 11%.
- PV₁ = 40,000 / 1.11 = 36,036.04
- PV₂ = 50,000 / (1.11)² = 50,000 / 1.2321 = 40,580.80
- PV₃ = 30,000 / (1.11)³ = 30,000 / 1.3676 = 21,936.24
- Total PV = 98,553.08
- NPV_H = 98,553.08 - 100,000 = **(-$1,446.92)**

**Step 3:** Apply Interpolation Formula.
$$ IRR = 10 + \frac{225}{225 - (-1446.92)} \times (11 - 10) $$

$$ IRR = 10 + \frac{225}{1671.92} \times 1 $$

$$ IRR = 10 + 0.1345 = 10.1345\% $$

> **Final Answer:** IRR = **10.13%**. Since 10.13% > MARR (10%), **Accept**.

---

# 9. Profitability Index (PI) ⭐⭐⭐

**Definition:** The ratio of the Present Value of future inflows to the Initial Investment. It shows the "Bang for the Buck."

**Formula:** 
$$ PI = \frac{\text{PV of Future Cash Inflows}}{\text{Initial Investment}} $$

**Decision Rule:**
- PI > 1 → Accept (Profit).
- PI < 1 → Reject (Loss).
- PI = 1 → Indifferent.

**Example (Using the same project above):**

> At 10%, PV of inflows = 100,225.39 (calculated in NPV example). Initial cost = 100,000.

$$ PI = \frac{100,225.39}{100,000} = 1.00225 $$

> **Final Answer:** PI = **1.002**. Since > 1, **Accept**.

# 10. Comparison of Techniques (Exam Cheat Sheet)

| Technique | Uses TVM? | Decision Rule | Gives Absolute Value? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Payback Period** | ❌ No | Lower is Better | No |
| **Discounted Payback** | ✅ Yes | Lower is Better | No |
| **NPV** | ✅ Yes | Higher is Better (NPV > 0) | **Yes ($)** |
| **IRR** | ✅ Yes | Higher is Better (IRR > MARR) | No (Percentage) |
| **PI** | ✅ Yes | Higher is Better (PI > 1) | No (Ratio) |

---

# 11. Conflicts Between Techniques (NPV vs IRR) ⭐⭐⭐

When evaluating **Mutually Exclusive Projects**, NPV and IRR might give conflicting accept/reject decisions.

**Why does the conflict happen?**
1. **Scale (Size) Difference:** Project A costs $1,000 and returns 50% (IRR high). Project B costs $1,000,000 and returns 20% (IRR lower, but NPV much higher). 
2. **Timing Difference:** Early cash flows vs. Late cash flows.

**The Ultimate Exam Rule:**
> **Always trust NPV over IRR.** IRR assumes cash flows are reinvested at the IRR rate (which is unrealistic). NPV assumes reinvestment at the cost of capital (MARR), which is more realistic.

# 18. Viva Questions (20 with Answers)

1. **Q:** Why do we add depreciation back to profits to get cash flow?  
   **A:** Depreciation is a non-cash expense. It reduces accounting profit but does not involve actual cash leaving the bank. For NPV, we care about actual cash.

2. **Q:** What happens to NPV if the discount rate increases?  
   **A:** NPV decreases. Higher discount rate lowers the PV of future inflows.

3. **Q:** Can IRR be negative?  
   **A:** Yes, if the project loses money and never recovers the investment.

4. **Q:** What is a "normal" cash flow pattern?  
   **A:** One negative initial flow followed by a series of positive inflows.

5. **Q:** What if a project has multiple IRRs?  
   **A:** Use NPV to make the decision. NPV is reliable regardless of sign changes.

6. **Q:** What is the reinvestment rate assumption of NPV?  
   **A:** It assumes reinvestment at the Cost of Capital (MARR).

7. **Q:** What is the reinvestment rate assumption of IRR?  
   **A:** It assumes reinvestment at the IRR rate (which is often unrealistic).

8. **Q:** Which technique is best for capital rationing?  
   **A:** Profitability Index (PI).

9. **Q:** What is the difference between Project Monitoring and Post-Completion Audit?  
   **A:** Monitoring is done *during* the project; Post-Audit is done *after* completion.

10. **Q:** Can Payback Period be zero?  
    **A:** Only if the initial investment is zero, which is unrealistic.

# 19. Common Mistakes (Exam Traps)

| ❌ Mistake | ✅ Correct Approach |
| :--- | :--- |
| Using Salvage Value as a Cost. | Salvage Value is a **Cash Inflow** at Year \(n\). |
| Forgetting to deduct Initial Cost in NPV. | Always do `Total PV - Cost`. |
| Choosing the highest IRR project regardless of size. | If mutually exclusive, choose highest **NPV**. |
| Using Undiscounted cash flows for DPP. | DPP requires **discounted** cash flows. |
| Ignoring the reinvestment rate assumption in conflicts. | Mention that **NPV** assumes realistic reinvestment. |
| Guessing IRR rates too close together. | Guess rates far apart (e.g., 10% and 20%) to make interpolation accurate. |
| Forgetting to add back depreciation in cash flow calculation. | Cash Flow = Net Income + Depreciation. |

# 20. Memory Tricks (Mnemonics)

- **"PIRP"** for Techniques: **P**ayback, **I**RR, **R**ecovery (Discounted PBP), **P**I, and **N**PV.
- **"N" is for "Now"** – NPV gives value in today's money.
- **"I" is for "Interest"** – IRR tells the interest rate you are earning.
- If NPV > 0, PI > 1, and IRR > MARR. They all move together.
- **"DOG"** for Capital Rationing: **D**ecide using **O**ptimal combination, **G**o with highest PI.

# 22. Mega "Golden" Combined Example (Full Marks)

> **Question:** A company is considering a project requiring an initial outlay of $200,000. The project is expected to generate the following net cash inflows:
> Year 1: $80,000, Year 2: $90,000, Year 3: $70,000, Year 4: $50,000.
> The required rate of return is 12%. 
> **Calculate:** Payback, Discounted Payback, NPV, IRR (approx.), and PI. Advise whether to accept.

---

**Step 1: Payback Period**
Cumulative: Y1=80k (Remaining -120k), Y2=90k (Remaining -30k), Y3=70k.
Fraction for Y3 = 30,000 / 70,000 = 0.428
**PBP = 2.43 Years.**

---

**Step 2: Discounted Payback (r=12%)**
- PV₁ = 80k / 1.12 = 71,428
- PV₂ = 90k / 1.2544 = 71,747
- PV₃ = 70k / 1.4049 = 49,824
- PV₄ = 50k / 1.5735 = 31,776

Cum PV: Y1=71,428 (-128,572); Y2=143,175 (-56,825); Y3=192,999 (-7,001); Y4=224,775.
Fraction Y4 = 7,001 / 31,776 = 0.22
**DPP = 3.22 Years.**

---

**Step 3: NPV**
Total PV of inflows = 224,775.
NPV = 224,775 - 200,000 = **$24,775**.
*(Since > 0 → Accept).*

---

**Step 4: Profitability Index**
PI = 224,775 / 200,000 = **1.124**.
*(Since > 1 → Accept).*

---

**Step 5: IRR (Approx Trial & Error)**
We know at 12%, NPV = +24,775. Try a higher rate (say 18%).
- PV₁ = 80k/1.18 = 67,796
- PV₂ = 90k/1.3924 = 64,636
- PV₃ = 70k/1.6430 = 42,605
- PV₄ = 50k/1.9388 = 25,789
- Total PV = 200,826.
- NPV at 18% = 200,826 - 200,000 = **+826**.

Try 19%:
- PV₁ = 80k/1.19 = 67,226
- PV₂ = 90k/1.4161 = 63,555
- PV₃ = 70k/1.6851 = 41,541
- PV₄ = 50k/2.0053 = 24,933
- Total PV = 197,255.
- NPV at 19% = 197,255 - 200,000 = **-2,745**.

Apply Interpolation:
$$ IRR = 18 + \frac{826}{826 - (-2745)} \times (19 - 18) $$
$$ IRR = 18 + \frac{826}{3571} = 18 + 0.23 = 18.23\% $$

*(Since 18.23% > 12% MARR → Accept).*
